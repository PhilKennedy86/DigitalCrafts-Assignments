HISTORY

The first internal combustion, petroleum fueled motorcycle was the Daimler Reitwagen. It was built by the German invetors Gottlieb Daimler and Wilhelm Maybach in Bad Cannstaff, Germany in 1885. The first commercial design for a self-propelled cycle was the three-wheel design called the Butler Petrol Cycle, conceived of Edward Butler in England in 1884. The vehicle was built by Merryweather Fire Engine company in Greenwich, in 1888.

First World War:

During WWI motorbike production was greatly ramped up for the war effort to supply effective communications with front line troops. This replaced messengers on horseback. Harley-Davidson, for the U.S. and Triumph, for Britain, were the main producers.

Postwar:

By 1920, U.S. manufacturer Harley-Davidson was the largest producer of motorcycles. By the later 1920s or early 1930s the German producer DKW took the lead as the largest producer. By the 1950s the design or steamlining began to play an increasing part in the development of racing motorcycles.
